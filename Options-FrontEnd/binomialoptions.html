Introduction

The Binomial Model provides a method to determine an option's fair price in present value by projecting future price movements of an underlying asset over a discrete amount of time. In its simplest form, the model assumes the asset price can move in only two directions — up or down — by a fixed magnitude.

Core Concepts

Five Key Components

  • Beginning Asset Value (S)
  • Up Move Factor (U) — the proportional gain when the asset price increases.
  • Down Move Factor (D) — the proportional loss when the asset price decreases, often defined as \(D = \frac{1}{U}\).
  • Strike Price (Sₖ) — the price at which the option may be exercised.
  • Risk-Free Rate (r) — a guaranteed rate of return with no risk.

Note

Although it may seem intuitive to assign probabilities to upward or downward price movements, we will later show that the option’s fair price is determined independently of real-world probabilities due to arbitrage considerations.

Forward Binomial Tree

Say the current stock price is \(P_0\) at time \(T_0\). From this point, it can only move up or down at the next time step \(T_1\).

We model this by adding two child nodes:
\(\text{Up} = P_0 \times U\)
\(\text{Down} = P_0 \times D\)

This process repeats recursively until expiration.

Backward Induction

European Options

At expiration, each terminal node has a payoff. Using these payoffs, we determine the fair option price today by working backward through the tree.

Step 1: Calculate the Payoffs

For a call option:
$$\text{Payoff} = \max(0,\; S - S_k)$$

For a put option:
$$\text{Payoff} = \max(0,\; S_k - S)$$

Step 2: Work Backward

At an earlier node (the parent of the two children), we compute the discounted expected value of the option.

$$V_{\text{current}} = e^{-r\,\Delta t}\left(pV_{\text{up}} + (1-p)V_{\text{down}}\right)$$

Here:
\(r\) is the risk-free rate
\(\Delta t\) is the time step
\(p\) is the risk-neutral probability

$$ p = \frac{e^{r \cdot \Delta t} - D}{U - D} $$

Note that instead of the probabilities given, P(U) or P(D), we use risk-neutral probability. This is because we want to ensure no arbitrage -- meaning there is no way to make a riskless profit by combining stocks and options. [Further Info]

Although it may be complicated, it'll be easier to understand once broken down. Inside the formula, it'll look identical as if you were to compute the regular expected value of the option, except we used risk-neutral probability instead of up and down probabilities. $$V_{current} = (p_{up} \cdot V_{\text{up}} + (1-p_{down}) \cdot V_{\text{down}})$$ The factor being multiplied by the expected value \(e^{-r \cdot Δt}\) is the discount factor. Since money is flowing from the future and closer to the present day, we must discount it so that it is equivalent in value. [Time Value of Money]

We'll repeat this process until we've reached the root node, which gives the fair option price today.

American Options

Key Differences

Whereas European options are only allowed to be exercised upon the date of expiration, American options can be exercised throughout their lifetime. Overall, this generally provides flexibility as the option can be exercised at any time to potentially achieve a greater profit (or loss).

Calculations

At each node at some time T, we ask one question: would the value of the option be if we exercised it? The immediate exercise value (\(IEV_t\)) answers that question. If you did exercise it at some time T, the immediate exercise value is simply the payoff you'd receive if you exercised the option right at that moment.

For a call option, the payoff at time T is as follows: $$IEV_t = max(0, S_t - S_k)$$ For a put option, the payoff at time T is as follows: $$IEV_t = max(S_k - S, 0)$$

Then there's the other possibility: what if we did not exercise it? Logically, its price would be identical to that of a European option, since at time T the option would not be exercised. We already know that value, the continuation value (\(CV_t\)), which is simply: $$CV_t = e^{-r \cdot Δt} \cdot (p \cdot V_{\text{up}} + (1-p) \cdot V_{\text{down}})$$

Now we're given two values to consider, the immediate exercise value and the continuation value. To determine the option's price at that price, we take the max of the two values: $$V_t = max(IET_t, CV_t)$$

At first glance, we take the max simply because a rational investor wants the higher payoff. But deeper than that, choosing anything other than the max creates an arbitrage opportunity.

Abritrage Example

If \(IEV_t\) = $15 and \(CV_t\) = $20 but the option is priced in at $15, an investor could buy it for $15 and hold it for a risk-free gain to $20. This guaranteed $5 profit violates no-arbitrage, so the option must be worth $20.
